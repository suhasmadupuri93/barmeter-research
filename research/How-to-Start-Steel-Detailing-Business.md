## 1. The Business in One Page

**What you sell:** You translate a structural engineer's design (the "intent") into precise, fabrication-ready shop drawings and connection designs that a steel fabricator and erector can actually build from. Every beam, column, plate, bolt, weld — drawn, dimensioned, numbered, and sequenced.

**Who buys it:**
- **Steel fabricators** (your #1 customer — 70–80% of the market)
- **General contractors** (when GCs procure detailing directly)
- **Structural engineers and architects** (less common — usually routed via the GC or fabricator)

**Why it exists as a service:** Detailing is highly specialized, software-heavy, and cyclical. Most fabricators don't keep a big in-house detailing team because the workload is uneven — they outsource peaks to firms like yours.

**How money flows:**
- You quote a price per **ton of structural steel** in the project (most common), or per drawing/hour.
- You deliver shop drawings, erection drawings, ABM (Anchor Bolt Material), gather sheets, CNC/KISS files, and coordinated 3D models.
- You handle **RFIs** (Requests for Information) when shop or field finds problems.

**Why it's a viable business:**
- Persistent demand — every steel building in the U.S. needs detailing
- Low capital startup (<$25K to launch solo from home)
- Strong offshore-friendly economics (India/Philippines production, U.S. sales)
- High repeat-business — fabricators want a detailer they trust

**Why it's hard:**
- Reputation-driven sales — your first 10 clients come from relationships, not marketing
- Technical learning curve (1–3 years to become genuinely competent)
- Liability risk if drawings are wrong (insurance is non-negotiable)
- Cyclical with U.S. nonresidential construction

## 2. Who the Clients Are (and What They Actually Buy)

### Tier 1 — Steel Fabricators (your bread and butter)

These are the steel **shops** that take raw mill steel (W-shapes, angles, plates) and turn it into the assembled members ready for site delivery. They are ~70–80% of the buying market for detailing services.

**What they buy from you:**
- Shop drawings — for each piece, what to cut/drill/weld
- Erection drawings — how the pieces go together on site
- Bolt summaries, ABM (anchor bolt material), gather sheets
- CNC / KISS export files for their automated saws and drill lines
- Coordinated 3D model (in SDS/2 or Tekla) they can use for production planning

**Sub-segments:**
- **AISC-certified fabricators** — bigger shops, do schools, hospitals, public-bid work. They want detailers who understand AISC certification requirements. Higher rates, more paperwork.
- **Non-certified small/regional shops** — strip malls, warehouses, light commercial. Smaller projects, faster cycles, often more price-sensitive.
- **Joist & deck specialists** — Vulcraft, Canam, etc. (rare entry point — usually in-house)
- **Bridge fabricators** — separate niche, separate certification (NSBA), separate detailing skill set

### Tier 2 — General Contractors (GCs)

GCs sometimes buy detailing directly — for example, on **design-build** or **construction-manager-at-risk (CMAR)** projects where they manage the steel package themselves and hire a fabricator on a labor-only basis.

**What they buy:**
- Same deliverables as fabricators
- Often, more emphasis on BIM coordination (clash detection vs MEP, architectural)
- More project management touchpoints

**Why this client matters:** Large GCs (Turner, Skanska, McCarthy, DPR, Whiting-Turner, Mortenson, Hensel Phelps) have enormous deal flow. Win one and you're set for years.

### Tier 3 — Structural Engineers & Architects

Rare direct buyers. When they do buy:
- Engineers buy **connection design** services to outsource the calc work
- Architects almost never buy detailing directly — they specify it as part of the steel package

### Tier 4 — Owners / Developers

Industrial owners (manufacturers, energy, data centers) sometimes hire detailers directly on **EPC (Engineer-Procure-Construct)** projects. Big checks, slow sales cycles, heavy procurement processes.

### Quick reference: typical buyer

| Buyer | % of detailing market | Project size | Sales cycle | Pricing power |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Steel fabricator | 70–80% | Small–large | 1–4 weeks | Medium |
| General contractor | 10–20% | Medium–large | 1–3 months | Lower (procurement) |
| Engineer / architect | <5% | Small | 1–2 weeks | Higher (technical sale) |
| Owner / developer | <5% | Large | 3–9 months | Lowest (RFP-driven) |

## 3. Why Clients Outsource Detailing

Five real reasons. Lead with these in your sales pitch.

1. **Workload is lumpy.** A fabricator might have 3 projects this month and 11 next month. Hiring a permanent detailing team for peak load is expensive — they'd rather outsource the peaks.
2. **Detailing is software-heavy.** SDS/2 + Tekla licenses, training, hardware, IT — a real cost a small shop doesn't want to carry.
3. **Talent is scarce in the U.S.** Senior U.S. detailers are aging out and there aren't enough new ones. Offshore detailing firms with trained teams fill the gap.
4. **Connection design needs a P.E.** Most fabricators don't have a Professional Engineer on staff licensed in every state — so they outsource the stamped connection design to firms (like Barmeter) that do.
5. **Schedule pressure.** When a job goes hot, the fabricator needs drawings *now*. A detailing partner with bench depth ships faster than scaling an internal team.

## 4. Pricing — How You Charge and What You Make

There are **three pricing models** in this industry. Most firms use a blend.

### Model A — Per ton of structural steel
**The dominant model.** You estimate the tonnage in the project, multiply by your rate.

| Region / firm type | Typical rate ($/ton) |
|---|---|
| Offshore (India/Philippines), commodity work | **$50–$80 / ton** |
| Offshore, premium (with connection design) | **$80–$120 / ton** |
| U.S.-based small firm | **$100–$180 / ton** |
| U.S.-based, complex projects (hospital, stadium) | **$200–$350+ / ton** |

> Industry source data point: small/freelance detailing typically charges **$85–$100 / tonne**, with project minimums often **$10,000–$15,000**. (See [Sources](#13-sources--resources).)

### Model B — Per drawing / per sheet
Used for small jobs or single-piece work. Roughly:
- Simple shop drawing: **$30–$80 / sheet**
- Complex erection drawing: **$80–$200 / sheet**

### Model C — Hourly (T&M)
Used for RFIs, change orders, and connection-design-only work.
- Junior modeler (offshore): **$15–$25 / hr**
- Senior modeler / checker: **$25–$45 / hr**
- U.S.-based detailer / engineer: **$60–$120 / hr**
- Stamped connection P.E.: **$120–$250 / hr**

### What clients actually pay (typical project examples)

| Project | Tonnage | Typical detailing fee |
|---|---|---|
| Small steel-frame building (warehouse) | 50 tons | $5,000–$10,000 |
| Mid-rise office | 500 tons | $50,000–$100,000 |
| Hospital wing (complex) | 1,500 tons | $250,000–$500,000 |
| Stadium / arena | 5,000+ tons | $1M+ |

### Add-ons / upsells (good margin)
- **Connection design (with P.E. stamp)** — separate fee, $500–$2,500 per state per project, or % of detailing fee
- **3D coordination / clash detection** — $5,000–$30,000
- **Erection sequencing** — $3,000–$15,000
- **Field support / on-site visits** — $1,500–$3,000 / day + travel
- **RFI handling** — usually included up to a cap, billed hourly above

## 5. Cost Structure & Realistic Margins

### Typical operating cost breakdown (% of revenue)

| Cost line | Solo detailer (home office) | Small firm (5–10 staff, U.S.) | Offshore-leveraged firm |
|---|---|---|---|
| Labor (modelers, checkers, engineers) | 15% | 55–65% | 35–45% |
| Software licenses | 5–8% | 4–6% | 2–4% |
| Insurance | 3–5% | 2–4% | 2–3% |
| P.E. stamping / engineering | 5–10% | 5–10% | 5–10% |
| Office, IT, admin | 5% | 8–12% | 4–6% |
| Sales & marketing | 2–5% | 3–6% | 4–8% |
| **Operating margin (EBITDA)** | **40–60%** | **15–25%** | **25–40%** |

### What this means
- A **solo detailer** working from home, billing 1,200–1,500 hours/year, can realistically clear **$80K–$180K/year** net.
- A **small U.S. firm** running $1.5M–$3M revenue typically nets $300K–$700K.
- A firm with **offshore production** (India/Philippines) and U.S. sales often runs the **best margins** — Barmeter's structure is exactly this.

## 6. How to Start — Solo Path vs Firm Path

### Path A — Solo / freelance (lowest risk)

**Time to first paid project:** 6–18 months (most of it learning the software)

**Ideal if:**
- You already have steel/CAD/structural background
- You want to test the market before quitting your day job
- You have a few personal contacts at fabricators

**Steps:**
1. Learn SDS/2 or Tekla (3–6 months serious time)
2. Build a portfolio (sample model, drawings, completed mock project)
3. Get NISD Individual Detailer Certification (eventually — see §7)
4. Buy minimum software (free Tekla student license to start; ~$2,500/yr when you commercialize)
5. LLC + insurance + business bank account
6. Land first 1–2 clients via personal network or subcontracting to an existing firm
7. Reinvest profits into more software, hiring offshore help, marketing

### Path B — Start a firm (Barmeter-style)

**Time to first revenue:** 3–9 months
**Capital required:** $50K–$250K depending on whether you bootstrap or hire upfront

**Ideal if:**
- You already have detailing experience (5+ years preferred)
- You have a few client commitments lined up before you start
- You can hire offshore production from day one (typical: India team of 2–5 modelers + checkers)

**Steps:**
1. Validate first 2–3 client commitments BEFORE incorporating
2. Set up entity (LLC or C-Corp), insurance ($1M–$5M E&O), business bank account
3. Hire offshore production team (Hyderabad/Bengaluru/Manila — $500–$1,500/mo per modeler all-in)
4. Establish QC process (a senior checker, even part-time, is non-negotiable)
5. Subcontract or hire a P.E. for connection design stamping (per-job basis to start)
6. Build a basic website and sales collateral
7. Run first 2–3 projects flawlessly — those become your case studies

## 7. Skills, Software & Certifications You Need

### Core technical skills
- **Structural steel literacy** — reading structural drawings, understanding loads, beam-column behavior at a working level (you don't need to be a structural engineer, but you need to read their drawings)
- **3D modeling proficiency** — SDS/2 or Tekla to a senior level
- **Geometry & trig** — for skews, miters, bevels, radiused work
- **AISC Steel Construction Manual** — your daily reference
- **AWS D1.1** (welding) and **AISC 360** (design specification) familiarity
- **Connection design fundamentals** — even if you sub the stamping work, you need to spec connections

### Software (and what it costs)

| Tool | Purpose | Cost (USD) |
|---|---|---|
| **SDS/2** | Detailing (US-favored) | ~$2,985/yr per seat (subscription); perpetual ~$15K + maintenance |
| **Tekla Structures** | Detailing (global standard) | ~$2,450–$3,500/yr per seat |
| **AutoCAD** | 2D legacy work | $1,955/yr |
| **Revit** | BIM coordination | $2,805/yr |
| **Bluebeam Revu** | Markup, RFI tracking | $300/yr |
| **RISA Connection / RISA 3D** | Connection design | $2,000–$4,000/yr |
| **Descon** | Connection design | $1,500–$3,000/yr |
| **Mathcad** | Calculation documentation | $700/yr |

**Hardware:** A solid 3D-capable workstation (32GB RAM, dedicated GPU) — about $1,500–$3,000 per seat.

### Certifications (in order of importance)

1. **NISD Individual Detailer Certification (IDC)** — National Institute of Steel Detailing
   - **Detailer (Class II):** 5+ yrs experience, or 3 yrs + training program
   - **Senior Detailer (Class I):** 10+ yrs experience with checking
   - **Disciplines:** Bridge or Structural/Miscellaneous
   - **Exam:** Open-book, ~8 hours, true/false + multi-choice + workout calculations
   - **Why it matters:** Signals competence to clients; required by some fabricators

2. **NISD Quality Procedures Program** — firm-level certification of your QC processes. Helpful for AISC fabricator clients.

3. **AISC Certification (for the firm)** — AISC certifies fabricators, but detailing firms can be **AISC-conforming** by aligning with their requirements. Major leverage with AISC-certified fab clients.

4. **P.E. License** — needed (or your sub-contracted engineer) if you offer stamped connection design. State-by-state — bigger U.S. coverage = bigger market.

5. **ISO 9001** — optional but credible; reportedly raises win rates 20–30% in 12 months for shops that get it.

### Insurance
- **Professional Liability / E&O** — $1M–$5M coverage. Premium: $2K–$10K/yr depending on revenue.
- **General Liability** — $1M–$2M. Premium: $500–$2,000/yr.
- **Cyber liability** — increasingly required by GCs. $500–$2,000/yr.

### Small firm startup budget (year 1)

| Item | Cost |

| Entity, legal, accounting setup | $5,000 |
| Software (3 seats + connection design tools) | $15,000 |
| Hardware (3 workstations) | $7,500 |
| 6 months runway, 3 offshore staff @ $1,200/mo | $21,600 |
| 1 senior U.S. detailer (part-time / contractor) | $30,000 |

| Sales & marketing | $15,000 |
| Buffer | $20,000 |
| **Total** | **~$120,000–$200,000** |

### Ongoing per-seat economics (offshore production)

A modeler in Hyderabad/Bengaluru fully loaded:
- Salary: $700–$1,500/mo
- Software: $250/mo
- Workstation amortized: $100/mo
- Office, IT, admin overhead: $300/mo
- **All-in cost: $1,500–$2,500/mo per seat**

That same seat can produce roughly **$5,000–$8,000/mo in billable work** (50–80 hrs at $25–$45 effective rate).

That's where the offshore margin comes from.

## 9. Sales Playbook — Finding & Winning Clients

### The truth about how detailing firms actually win clients
This is a **relationship-driven, reputation-driven** business. Marketing matters, but the first 10 clients almost always come from one of:
- **Personal contacts** from prior jobs
- **Subcontracting** for an established detailing firm
- **Referrals** from a happy client
- **Cold outreach** to fabricators with a credible portfolio

You will rarely win a project from a website inquiry alone in year 1.

### The 5-step approach that works

**Step 1 — Pick a beachhead.**
Don't try to serve everyone. Pick a niche:
- Geography (e.g., Texas fabricators)
- Vertical (e.g., AISC-certified hospital work, or industrial / data center)
- Service angle (e.g., complex stairs and rails specialist; or stamped connections only)

**Step 2 — Build a reference project.**
Take one job — paid, free, or from a friend's shop — and execute it flawlessly. Document tonnage, schedule met, RFI count, drawings count. **This becomes your first case study.**

**Step 3 — Make a target list of 50 fabricators in your beachhead.**
Use the AISC certified-fabricator search, your state's fabricator association, and LinkedIn. Capture: company name, owner/PM name, email, phone, what work they do.

**Step 4 — Outreach in three waves.**
- **Wave 1 (LinkedIn connect + short message):** Personal, no pitch. "Saw you finished [Project X] — that's the kind of work I support detailing on. Mind if we connect?"
- **Wave 2 (email, ~7 days later):** Short — who you are, one case study link, one specific question. *"What does your detailing pipeline look like for Q3?"*
- **Wave 3 (call):** Phone call to anyone who replied, plus a polite follow-up to the rest.

**Step 5 — Always be doing one paid sample.**
Offer to detail a few connections, a stair, or a small package on a job for free or at cost — to prove competence. This converts way better than a brochure.

### What to put in your sales pitch
1. Your sequence-ready / RFI-reducing process (the Barmeter angle)
2. Your tools (SDS/2 + Tekla)
3. Your QC workflow (this matters MORE to fabricators than software does)
4. Your turnaround time on a typical project
5. Your stamping coverage (what states is your P.E. licensed in)
6. References — even one is enough

### Sample cold-email template
```
Subject: Detailing capacity for your Q3 pipeline?

Hi [Name],

Saw [Fabricator] is doing [Project Type] in [City]. Nice work.

I run [Firm Name] — we do steel detailing and connection
design for fabricators in [Region]. Our turnaround on a
typical 200–500 ton project is 2–3 weeks, with a checker
review on every package before it ships.

A recent example: [1-line case study with metric].

If your detailing bench is tight for Q3, would love to
chat for 15 minutes. Otherwise, no worries — file me
under "for next time."

[Your name]
[Phone] · [Website]
```

## 10. Where to Find Clients (Channels & Lists)

### Direct sources for finding U.S. fabricators
- **AISC Find a Fabricator** — searchable directory of AISC-certified fabricators (the most credible buying pool)
- **State / regional fabricator associations** — Texas Steel Erectors Association, Michigan SCA, etc.
- **AGC (Associated General Contractors)** member directories — the GCs who manage steel packages
- **ENR (Engineering News-Record) Top Lists** — Top 400 GCs, Top Specialty Contractors
- **Dodge Construction Network** (paid) — bid leads
- **Construction Connect / iSqFt / BuildingConnected** (paid) — project bid pipelines
- **LinkedIn** — search "Project Manager" + "[fabricator name]" or "Estimator" + "structural steel"

### Industry events (high ROI for relationships)
- **NASCC: The Steel Conference** (AISC, annual) — the single biggest steel-industry event in the U.S.
- **AISC fabricator/erector regional events**
- **AGC conventions**
- **Greenbuild, Build Expo, AIA Conference** (architect side)

### Online presence (for inbound, slowly)
- A clean website with case studies, services, and a quote form
- LinkedIn page with regular posts on projects (anonymized if needed) and tips
- Google Business Profile (esp. for state-targeting)
- Trade publication features in *Modern Steel Construction* (AISC's magazine), *STRUCTURE Magazine*

### Paid lead sources (used carefully)
- BuildingConnected, iSqFt, Construction Connect — useful for bid leads; expensive monthly subscriptions
- Cad Crowd, Upwork — low-rate freelance work to start; not a long-term strategy

## 11. Roadmap — Month 0 to Year 3

### Month 0–6 — Learn & build foundation
- Train on SDS/2 or Tekla (free Tekla student license; SDS/2 trial)
- Complete 1–2 sample projects (full model + drawings)
- Form LLC, get insurance, set up bookkeeping
- Register for NISD certification (if eligible)
- Build a one-page website and LinkedIn presence

### Month 6–12 — First clients
- Start subcontracting to an established detailing firm (paid learning)
- Cold-outreach to your 50-fabricator target list
- Land first 1–3 paying projects
- Document everything as case studies

### Year 1–2 — Stabilize
- Recurring work from 2–4 fabricators
- Hire first offshore modeler ($1,000–$1,500/mo)
- Sub a P.E. for connection design stamping
- Get NISD certification if you don't have it
- Attend NASCC for the first time — meet 20 fabricators in 3 days

### Year 2–3 — Scale
- Build to 5–10 staff (U.S. + offshore)
- Add 2–3 more state P.E. coverage
- Pursue AISC-certified fabricator clients (higher value)
- Start specializing — pick 1–2 verticals to be known for
- Move from $/ton commodity pricing to value-based pricing on complex work

## 12. Common Pitfalls & Hard-Won Lessons

1. **Underpricing on the first big project.** Once you're cheap with a client, you stay cheap. Quote what you'd quote project #5.
2. **No checker = disaster.** Every package needs an experienced checker before it ships. A single bad shipment of drawings can cost a fabricator $50K+ in shop rework — you'll lose the client.
3. **Saying yes to everything.** A complex hospital project will eat a firm that's used to warehouses. Decline work outside your competence; refer it.
4. **No insurance.** If a connection design fails and someone gets hurt, you lose the business and possibly your house.
5. **Over-leverage on offshore in year 1.** Quality-control distance is real. Have a strong U.S.-side checker before scaling production overseas.
6. **Ignoring RFI workflow.** Slow RFI responses kill jobs and reputations. Have a 24-hour acknowledgement standard, in writing.
7. **Chasing GCs before you can serve fabricators.** GCs have heavier procurement processes. Fabricators are faster decisions and bigger volume.
8. **Skipping NISD/AISC alignment.** Without these, you're locked out of the higher-margin certified work.
